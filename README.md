## Cold Calling Anxiety Tips That Complement the Audio

The audio rewires your subconscious. But there are things you can do during your workday that amplify the effect. Here's what actually helps, based on what experienced sales reps consistently report:

**1. Stop calling it "cold calling."**

The program mentions this, and it's worth repeating. The word "cold" primes your brain for rejection. Nobody wants something cold. C. McNeil suggests changing the framing — call it outreach, connecting, or just "doing your job." One word shift. Sounds trivial. But language shapes perception, and perception shapes action.

**2. Build a pre-call ritual.**

Not a complicated one. Something small that signals to your brain: "We're doing this now." A specific playlist. A two-minute breathing exercise. A particular phrase you say out loud. The ritual becomes a trigger — like Pavlov's bell, but for confidence.

**3. Set time blocks, not call quotas.**

"Make 50 calls today" is overwhelming before you start. "Dial for 25 minutes, then take a break" is manageable. Your brain can handle 25 minutes of almost anything. After the first block, the anxiety usually drops because you've already survived it.

**4. Record yourself (yes, really).**

Not to cringe at your voice. Do it to notice that you sound more competent than you feel. Most people with call anxiety have a distorted self-perception. Hearing yourself on playback often reveals that you're actually doing fine — your internal panic isn't leaking through the way you think it is.

**5. Detach from the outcome.**

A Reddit thread on r/sales had a gem: "Remove any attachment to the outcome. Focus on delivering the script in the best way possible and listening with genuine curiosity." Your job isn't to close on every call. It's to show up, be present, and let the conversation go where it goes.

These tips work. But here's the catch — they all require conscious effort. That's why they pair well with the audio. The audio handles the wiring. The tips handle the execution. Together, they cover both sides of the equation.
